Honey Bees

Use of Honeybees

A honey bee (also spelled honeybee) is a eusocial flying insect from the genus Apis of the largest bee family, Apidae. Honey bees are known for their construction of perennial nests within cavities (i.e. beehives) containing hexagonal cells made of secreted wax, their large colony sizes, and their routine regurgitation of digested carbohydrates as surplus food storage in the form of honey, the lattermost of which distinguishes their hives as a prized foraging target of many mellivorous animals including honey badgers, bears and human hunter-gatherers.

Beekeeping, or apiculture is the maintenance of bee colonies, commonly in artificial beehives. Honey bees in the genus Apis are the most commonly kept species but other honey producing bees such as Melipona stingless bees are also kept. Beekeepers (or apiarists) keep bees to collect honey and other products of the hive: beeswax, propolis, bee pollen, and royal jelly. Other sources of beekeeping income include pollination of crops, raising queens, and production of package bees for sale. Bee hives are kept in an apiary or "bee yard".

Why Honey Bees are Important?

Honeybees produce honey, beeswax, propolis, royal jelly, and bee bread to sustain their hive and these products are valuable for humans. They create honey by concentrating flower nectar through enzymatic activity and evaporation. Beeswax is secreted to build honeycomb, while propolis (tree resin) and royal jelly are used for hive maintenance and feeding larvae, respectively.
